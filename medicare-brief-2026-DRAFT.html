Universal Coverage

Who Is Covered?

This bill extends Medicare to all lawful U.S. residents — regardless of age, employment, or income. Enrollment is automatic; no application required.

45% Gross Receipts Rule

Private insurers are not eligible for Medicare payments unless at least 45% of their gross receipts come from Medicare — preventing public funds from subsidizing primarily private-market insurers. This applies to all providers including hospitals, physician groups, and all other entities.

This threshold ensures insurers remain substantially committed to the public program — not treating Medicare as a marginal revenue supplement. Insurers can't cherry-pick Medicare payments while primarily serving lucrative private markets.

Cost

Funding Mechanism

The U.S. already spends $5.3 trillion on health care annually — $15,474 per person, nearly double what peer nations spend per capita. The debate is not about whether we spend enough, but whether we spend it efficiently.

$5.3T
U.S. total health spending, 2024
CMS National Health Expenditure Accounts · 2024

The U.S. spends ~$5,000 more per person than Switzerland, the next highest-spending peer nation. The CBO projects ~$400B/year in administrative savings by reducing private insurer overhead (~12%) to Medicare's level (~2%).

Proposed Enhancements to Medicare Funding

1
Income-Based Tax (MAGI): Individuals pay a percentage of Modified Adjusted Gross Income — capturing all income sources, not only wages. This means additional contributions apply if you have income beyond employment (investments, rental income, etc.).
2
Organizational Gross Income Contribution: All private and public organizations contribute a TBD% of gross income, subject to a minimum income threshold. Both private and public organizations are included.
3
Employee-Count Surcharge: Companies pay an additional contribution based on number of employees — removing the financial incentive to hire part-time or low-wage workers. Note: ~64% of Medicaid-covered individuals are employed, making employer accountability central to equitable financing.

"Federal spending could increase while national health spending decreases, if a federal program takes over private spending and does it at lower cost."

— Urban Institute, Health Spending Analysis

Enrollment & Transition

Enrollment Timeline

The bill prioritizes the most vulnerable Americans immediately, then opens opt-in windows before mandating universal enrollment in Year 3.

Day 1 — Immediate Auto-Enrollment
Automatic coverage for all uninsured individuals, every newborn, and adults age 55+. Zero gaps from enactment. No application required. Covers those with no coverage to disrupt, newborns from birth, and adults approaching Medicare eligibility who face high health care costs.
Years 1–2 — Employer Market Opt-In Window
Employer-sponsored plan participants (~160M enrollees) receive a structured voluntary opt-in window — preserving choice during the transition period. Employers must notify all employees within 90 days. Individuals who opt in receive Medicare coverage the following month.
Year 3 — Universal Mandate
All remaining lawful residents automatically enrolled. Medicaid and CHIP integrate into the Medicare program. Employer-sponsored health insurance plan mandates sunset and employer contributions redirect to Medicare financing. Full universal coverage achieved.
VA and IHS Unaffected
The Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) and Indian Health Service (IHS) are not affected by this Act and continue operating independently.
Non-Discrimination Protections

Equal Access, Enforced by Law

The bill incorporates and strengthens civil rights frameworks — prohibiting discrimination at both the provider and plan-administrator level, a meaningful expansion beyond prior law.

⚖️
ACA Section 1557 — Anti-Discrimination Baseline
Incorporates the existing ACA prohibition on discrimination in any federally funded health program — covering race, color, national origin, sex, age, and disability. Now applied to the expanded Medicare program. This is the established federal baseline, not a new provision.
Source: Medicare for America Act (2019) · ACA §1557
🏥
Providers AND Health Plans Both Bound
Prior law often only bound the treating provider (the doctor or hospital). This bill explicitly prohibits discrimination by health plans and administrators — meaning the insurer or plan cannot deny, delay, or limit coverage on discriminatory grounds. A patient treated equitably by their physician cannot face discriminatory denials from their insurer. Both levels of the system are held accountable simultaneously.
Scope: Dual accountability — clinical + administrative
📋
Expanded Protected Categories
Spells out an explicit list of characteristics one cannot be discriminated against — removing vagueness in current law. Includes race, language, pregnancy, gender identity, age, disability, and more. The Secretary may add additional categories by regulation to reflect evolving civil rights standards.
Effect: Removes vagueness — names specific protected categories
🛡️
Robust Enforcement
HHS has independent authority to investigate and penalize violators. The bill allows both governmental and individual lawsuits — ensuring accountability at every level. The Attorney General and State attorneys general may also bring civil actions.
Mechanism: HHS authority + private right of action + governmental suits
Feasibility of Implementation

Can It Be Done?

Implementation poses real challenges but has historical precedent: Medicare launched in just 11 months in 1966, and Canada transitioned to universal coverage over a decade. Over 30 nations operate universal systems today — all achieving better outcomes at lower per-capita cost than the U.S.

30+
Nations with universal coverage
All achieve better outcomes at lower per-capita cost than the U.S.

Administrative Savings

Private insurer overhead runs ~12% of revenue vs. Medicare's ~2%. The CBO projects ~$400B/year in savings from consolidation. Providers also benefit: hospital admin costs fall from 19% to ~12% of revenue; physician administrative overhead falls from 15% to ~9%; nurses spend 18.4% less time on administrative activities (CBO).

Key Risks to Acknowledge

◆
Provider payment disruption during transition — Medicare reimburses below private rates; rural hospitals and specialists face potential revenue shortfalls.
◆
IT system consolidation — health systems mid-transition from manual to electronic quality reporting face additional complexity. FHIR-based interoperability mandates would mitigate fragmentation risk.
◆
Workforce dislocation — approximately 1.8M insurance industry jobs would be displaced (PERI). The bill includes provisions for workforce transition assistance.
Value-Based Program Continuity
All existing ACA and Medicare value-based programs continue operating as-is. They sunset only once the new federal quality reporting structure is fully implemented and operationalized — no abrupt disruption to existing programs.
Accountable Care Organizations

What Is an ACO?

An Accountable Care Organization (ACO) is a group of doctors, hospitals, and other health care providers who voluntarily come together to give coordinated, high-quality care to a defined population of patients. ACOs are not insurance plans — patients retain full rights under Original Medicare and may see any provider that accepts Medicare, even outside the ACO.

How ACOs Work

ACOs shift incentives from fee-for-service (pay per procedure) to value-based care (pay for outcomes). Providers are accountable for both the quality and total cost of care for their attributed patient population.

1
Coordinate care — Providers share patient data via electronic health records to prevent duplicated services, medication errors, and unnecessary ER visits.
2
Meet quality benchmarks — ACOs are measured against ~30 quality metrics across patient experience, care coordination, safety, and preventive health.
3
Share in savings — When spending falls while quality is maintained, the ACO receives a share of those savings. Poor performance can result in financial penalties.
4
Address social determinants — ACOs examine housing, food security, and other factors, connecting patients with community supports to close equity gaps.

ACOs in This Bill

85% P4P Pass-Through Mandate
At least 85% of Pay-for-Performance payments must flow directly to individual providers — not group administrators — ensuring incentives reach the clinicians delivering care.

Regional Administration

Multi-State Regions

Quality of care is tracked through ACOs organized at both the multi-state regional and individual state level, with states accountable to a regional entity regardless of which path they choose.

◆
Tiered, Flexible Structure: Regional and state-level ACOs each have a designated director, annual health assessment, and the autonomy to collaborate or operate independently. States may join a multi-state regional ACO or operate their own state-level ACO — but all remain accountable to a regional entity.
◆
System-Neutral ACOs: No ACO may be administered by or affiliated with a single healthcare system — preserving patient choice and preventing market consolidation by large health systems.
◆
National Quality Floor: All ACOs report quality metrics federally, ensuring a consistent national standard with local flexibility in how care is delivered and organized. This creates natural peer benchmarking between regions — driving quality improvement through comparison.

Why a Multi-Regional Framework?

◆
Prevents single point of failure — distributes administrative, political, and financial risk across regions rather than concentrating it nationally.
◆
Matches how health care is actually delivered — patient populations, disease burdens, provider supply, and cost structures vary dramatically by region. Local ACOs reflect those realities.
◆
Builds on existing infrastructure — Vermont's OneCare, Oregon's Coordinated Care Organizations, and others can be absorbed and expanded rather than dismantled.
◆
Drives competitive quality improvement — regional benchmarking creates visible, accountable comparisons that incentivize improvement above the national floor.
